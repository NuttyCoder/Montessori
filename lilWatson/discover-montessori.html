Dr. Maria Montessori - The History of Montessori

In 1906, Dr. Maria Montessori, an Italian educator, physician, and scientist, who had just judged an international competition on the subjects of scientific pedagogy and experimental psychology, was invited to create a childcare center in San Lorenzo, a poor, inner-city district of Rome. There, she would be working with some of the area’s most disadvantaged, and previously unschooled, children.
She opened the doors on January 6, 1907, calling the center the Casa dei Bambini—Italian for “Children’s House.” Dr. Montessori was determined to make the Casa a quality educational environment for these youngsters, whom many had thought were unable to learn—and she did.
While the children were unruly at first, they soon showed great interest in working with puzzles, learning to prepare meals and clean their environment, and engaging in hands-on learning experiences. Dr. Montessori observed that before long, the children exhibited calm, peaceful behavior, periods of deep concentration, and a sense of order in caring for their environment. She saw that the children absorbed knowledge from their surroundings, essentially teaching themselves. Utilizing scientific observation and experience gained from her earlier work with young children, Dr. Montessori designed unique learning materials for them, many of which are still in use in Montessori classrooms today, and created a classroom environment that fostered the children’s natural desire to learn.
News of the school’s success soon spread through Italy. On April 7, 1907, Dr. Montessori opened a second Casa dei Bambini, also in San Lorenzo. And on October 18, 1907, in Milan, she opened a third Casa.

Here are 20 facts about Dr. Maria Montessori:

Doctor Before Educator: Before her work in education, Dr. Maria Montessori was one of the first women to graduate from medical school in Italy, earning her degree in 1896. Focused on Special Needs: Her early career included working with children with special needs, which significantly influenced her educational methods. Feminist Advocate: Montessori was an advocate for women's rights and was part of the feminist movement, campaigning for gender equality and women's empowerment in Italy. Global Influence: Dr. Montessori's educational philosophy spread worldwide, with schools established in every continent except Antarctica. Exiled from Italy: Due to her opposition to fascism, particularly under Mussolini, she was forced into exile from Italy during World War II. Nominated for Nobel Prize: She was nominated for the Nobel Peace Prize three times, although she never won the award. Anthropological Studies: Montessori conducted extensive studies in anthropology, applying her findings to understand child development better. Montessori's Son Collaborated with Her: Her son, Mario Montessori, worked closely with her in developing and promoting the Montessori Method, continuing her work after her death. Observation-Based Method: Her teaching approach was heavily based on observation and experimentation, focusing on creating an environment that encouraged independent learning. Developed Didactic Materials: She invented specific didactic materials designed to promote self-directed learning among children. Peace Education: Montessori emphasized the importance of educating for peace and believed that education was a critical factor in achieving world peace. Opened First School in 1907: The first Montessori school, Casa dei Bambini, opened in Rome in 1907 and was initially established for children in a low-income housing project. Influenced by Environmental Design: She was one of the first educators to stress the importance of a prepared environment tailored to children's learning needs. Spiritual Approach to Learning: Montessori believed in a more spiritual and holistic approach to education, viewing children as having potential beyond the physical realm. Refuge to India: During World War II, she took refuge in India, where she continued her work and greatly influenced Indian educational practices. Patented Materials: Montessori secured patents for some educational materials, underscoring her innovative approach to child-centered learning. Influence on Modern Education: Her ideas have permeated modern educational practices, influencing various teaching methods beyond the Montessori schools. Shift from Medicine to Education: Despite her medical background, she shifted her focus to education after becoming fascinated with children’s learning processes. Studied Education Barriers: Montessori was interested in studying and breaking down barriers in education, particularly those related to societal status and ability. Enduring Legacy: Montessori schools continue to thrive globally, with her approach being utilized in new and innovative ways in the 21st century.

Montessori is a method of education that is based on self-directed activity, hands-on learning and collaborative play. In Montessori classrooms, children make creative choices in their learning, while the classroom and the teacher offer age-appropriate activities to guide the process.

Children work in groups and individually to discover and explore knowledge of the world and to develop their maximum potential. The Montessori Method is designed to take full advantage of the children's desire to learn and their unique ability to develop their own capabilities.

Montessori teachers act as guides, observing and facilitating the children's learning. They are trained to recognize the needs of each child and to direct the child's learning in a non-intrusive way. They are also trained to recognize when it is time to step back and let the child take the lead.
